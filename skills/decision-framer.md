# Decision Framer

A skill that helps you make better decisions by systematically reasoning through
options, trade-offs, and criteria — then giving you a clear, justified recommendation.

## What It Does

Decision Framer goes far beyond "here are the pros and cons." It:
- Identifies your real decision criteria (not just the obvious ones)
- Maps out all viable options (including ones you might not have considered)
- Reasons for and against each option from multiple perspectives
- Surfaces hidden trade-offs and second-order consequences
- Gives you a concrete recommendation with a rationale
- Tells you what to watch for after you decide

This is the replacement for asking ChatGPT "what should I do?" and getting a
wishy-washy non-answer that doesn't actually help.

## How to Use

1. Type `/decision-framer` in Claude
2. Describe the decision you're trying to make — as much or as little context as you have
3. Answer any clarifying questions Claude asks
4. Receive a structured decision analysis and a recommendation

## Example

> **User:** `/decision-framer` — I'm thinking about shifting completely to Claude
> and abandoning ChatGPT and other tools like Gemini. Is that the right choice,
> or should I do something else?
>
> **Decision Framer:**
>
> **The Real Decision:** Not "Claude vs. ChatGPT" but "What's my AI stack strategy?"
>
> **Options Mapped:**
> 1. Claude as primary, keep one backup
> 2. Keep all three, use by task type
> 3. Go all-in on Claude, kill the duplication
>
> **Decision Criteria:** Cost, workflow friction, capability gaps, future-proofing
>
> **Reasoning:** *(for/against each option)*
>
> **Recommendation:** Claude as primary, one backup for specific gaps (e.g., Gemini
> for Workspace integration). Going all-in risks losing capability coverage;
> keeping all three creates decision fatigue and cost overhead.
>
> **Watch-Outs:** Claude's context window limits on long projects; re-evaluate in 90 days.

## Framework Used

Decision Framer applies a combination of:
- **MECE option mapping** — Mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive options
- **Criteria weighting** — What actually matters for this decision
- **Pre-mortem thinking** — What could go wrong with each option
- **Reversibility check** — Is this a one-way or two-way door?
- **Regret minimization** — What would you regret most at age 80?

## Best For

- Career and job decisions
- Business strategy choices
- Tool and software stack decisions
- Major purchases or investments
- Life decisions (city, relationship, education)
- Product and feature prioritization
- Founder dilemmas and pivot decisions

## Output Structure

1. **Reframing the Decision** — What you're actually deciding
2. **Your Options** — Full map including non-obvious alternatives
3. **Decision Criteria** — What matters and why
4. **Analysis** — For/against each option against each criterion
5. **Recommendation** — Clear, direct, with reasoning
6. **Watch-Outs** — What to monitor after you decide
7. **Revisit Trigger** — When to re-examine this decision

## Tips

- Give more context for better analysis — what's your situation, constraints, goals?
- Tell Claude what you're leaning toward — it will steelman the alternatives
- Ask "what am I not considering?" for a second-pass on blind spots
- Use it for team decisions too — share the output to align stakeholders
